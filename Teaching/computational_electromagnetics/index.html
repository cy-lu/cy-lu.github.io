4. Main numerical methods

No numerical method is universally best. The correct choice depends on the mathematical structure of the problem: whether the geometry is layered or fully three-dimensional, whether the quantity of interest is broadband or single-frequency, whether local field resolution is important, and whether the material response is dispersive, lossy, anisotropic, or strongly resonant.

In practice, the three methods most relevant here are the transfer-matrix method (TMM), the finite-difference time-domain method (FDTD), and the finite element method (FEM). They should not be viewed as competing software choices, but as different mathematical tools with different domains of validity.

Practical rule: choose the simplest method that still matches the physics and the geometry.

5. Transfer-matrix method (TMM)

The transfer-matrix method is the natural starting point for planar multilayer media. It treats each layer as a homogeneous slab and enforces field continuity at interfaces. The full optical response is obtained by multiplying interface and propagation matrices in sequence.

What TMM is mathematically

TMM is essentially a one-dimensional wave-matching method. It assumes that the structure varies along the propagation direction, while the transverse directions are uniform. For this reason, it is very efficient and physically transparent.

When TMM is strong

  • thin-film optics and multilayer coatings,
  • metal–dielectric absorber stacks,
  • Fabry–Pérot resonators,
  • anti-reflection design,
  • wavelength-dependent reflectance, transmittance, and absorption in layered media.

Why it is valuable pedagogically

TMM is one of the best teaching tools in computational electromagnetics because it exposes the physics directly: phase accumulation, impedance mismatch, interference, and resonance can all be seen without the overhead of a full-wave grid solver.

Limits of TMM

  • It is not a general full-wave solver for arbitrary geometry.
  • It breaks down once the structure is no longer effectively one-dimensional.
  • It cannot naturally handle lateral patterning, isolated particles, curved interfaces, or arbitrary defects.
  • Local near-field distributions in complex nanostructures are outside its natural scope.
  • Once periodicity or patterning becomes essential, methods such as FDTD, FEM, or RCWA are more appropriate.

In short, TMM is excellent for layered structures, but it should not be stretched beyond that class of problems.

6. Finite-difference time-domain (FDTD)

FDTD solves Maxwell’s curl equations directly in the time domain on the Yee grid. The electric and magnetic field components are staggered in space and time, and are updated explicitly in a leapfrog scheme.

What FDTD is mathematically

FDTD is a direct time-marching discretization of Maxwell’s equations. Because it works in time, a single pulsed simulation can produce a broad frequency response after Fourier transformation. This is one of its main strengths.

When FDTD is strong

  • broadband scattering and absorption problems,
  • transient wave propagation,
  • near-field and far-field analysis,
  • pulse-driven systems,
  • nanophotonic structures with strong field localization,
  • situations where time evolution itself is physically informative.

Why FDTD is important

FDTD behaves like a numerical laboratory for wave propagation. It shows how waves enter, scatter, reflect, interfere, and decay in time. For teaching and physical intuition, this is extremely powerful.

Limits of FDTD

  • The spatial grid is global, so the entire domain pays the cost of the smallest required mesh size.
  • Fine geometric detail or subwavelength features can make memory and runtime extremely expensive.
  • The time step is restricted by the Courant stability condition, so finer spatial resolution immediately increases cost.
  • Numerical dispersion can distort phase velocity and resonance positions if the mesh is not sufficiently fine.
  • Curved or oblique interfaces are approximated on a Cartesian grid unless subpixel or conformal techniques are used.
  • PML setup, TF/SF injection, and spectral extraction can all introduce hidden errors if handled carelessly.

7. Finite element method (FEM)

FEM solves the governing equations in weak form by discretizing the domain into finite elements and approximating the solution with basis functions. In electromagnetics, FEM is often used in the frequency domain, though time-domain variants also exist.

What FEM is mathematically

FEM is a variational method. Instead of updating fields point by point on a structured grid, it solves a global system assembled from local element contributions. This makes it especially suitable for complex geometry and nonuniform meshing.

When FEM is strong

  • complex CAD-based geometry,
  • curved boundaries and nonuniform domains,
  • local mesh refinement near corners, gaps, ports, or singular features,
  • frequency-domain resonators and eigenmode problems,
  • multiphysics coupling, including thermal, structural, or electrostatic interaction.

Why FEM is often preferred in engineering simulation

FEM is usually the most flexible method when geometry matters more than broadband speed. It is particularly strong when one needs to resolve a small critical region inside a larger device without refining the whole domain.

Limits of FEM

  • Broadband analysis is usually less natural than in FDTD because separate frequency points often require separate solves.
  • For very large 3D full-wave problems, the global matrix system can become memory-intensive.
  • Open-region radiation problems require careful treatment of absorbing boundaries or infinite elements.
  • The method is less intuitive for visualizing time evolution unless a time-domain FEM formulation is specifically used.
  • Poor mesh quality or inappropriate basis order can create numerical errors that are less visually obvious than in FDTD.

In short, FEM is the strongest choice when the problem is geometrically complex, locally refined, or multiphysics-coupled, but it is not automatically the most efficient method for broadband wave problems.
