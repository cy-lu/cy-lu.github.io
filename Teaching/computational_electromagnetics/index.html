Computational Electromagnetics

Two-week teaching page on Maxwell solvers, observables, material models, and validation.

This page is built as a course page, not as a brochure. The logic is: physics → discretization → solver → observable → validation. The aim is to teach students how to think, not just how to run software.

Core principle. A field plot is not a result. A result is a defensible observable extracted from a discretized Maxwell problem whose assumptions, resolution limits, and validation path are all explicit.

2. Maxwell equations and constitutive relations

All computational electromagnetics starts from Maxwell’s equations. What changes from solver to solver is how these equations are approximated and what class of geometry or observable is targeted.

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}, \] \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0. \]

For linear isotropic media:

\[ \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon \mathbf{E}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}, \qquad \mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{E}. \]

In optics and broadband RF, the medium is usually dispersive, so \(\varepsilon\) becomes \(\varepsilon(\omega)\), and the constitutive relation becomes a real modeling decision rather than a fixed constant.

3. Observables, not decorative field maps

Raw fields are necessary, but they are almost never the final answer. The actual target is an observable that can support interpretation, design, or validation.

Reflection / Transmission

Used in coatings, filters, metasurfaces, waveguides, and slab problems.

S-parameters

Natural language of microwave and port-based devices.

Cross sections

Scattering, absorption, and extinction for particles, radar, and nano-optics.

Q and eigenfrequency

Resonators, cavities, filters, oscillators, and photonic structures.

\[ A = 1 - R - T \]
Do not confuse visibility with validity. A bright hot spot in \(|\mathbf{E}|\) may say little about matching, efficiency, or loss unless the observable extraction is mathematically correct.
Figure 2. Field map versus observable
The student must learn to move from raw fields to a metric that can be compared across meshes, solvers, or experiments.

4. How to choose a solver

The correct question is not “which method is best?” but “which method matches the structure, the bandwidth, the source condition, and the observable?”

Method Best matched problem class Main strength Main penalty Minimal classroom example
TMM 1D multilayers Fast, analytical structure, exact for planar stacks No lateral patterning Quarter-wave anti-reflection coating
RCWA Periodic layered gratings Efficient for diffraction and periodic nanophotonics Convergence can be delicate for high contrast features 1D dielectric grating spectrum
FDTD Broadband transient full-wave problems One pulse gives broadband response; strong intuition in time Global grid cost; CFL constraint Pulse incident on dielectric slab
FEM Complex geometry, resonators, multiphysics Local mesh refinement; flexible geometry Broadband sweep means repeated solves Waveguide bend or cavity eigenmode
MoM Open-region radiation / conducting scatterers Radiation handled naturally through Green’s functions Dense matrices; harder with inhomogeneous volumetric media Thin-wire dipole current
A good course page must expose this selection logic clearly. Otherwise students reduce method choice to software familiarity, which is exactly the wrong habit.

5. Transfer-matrix method (TMM)

Method idea

Planar multilayers

TMM solves 1D stratified media by propagating forward and backward wave amplitudes through each layer. It is the cleanest entry point for teaching interference, impedance matching, and thin-film spectra.

\[ \begin{bmatrix} E_0^+ \\ E_0^- \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{M}_1 \mathbf{M}_2 \cdots \mathbf{M}_N \begin{bmatrix} E_{N+1}^+ \\ E_{N+1}^- \end{bmatrix} \]

Minimal example: quarter-wave anti-reflection coating

  • Medium 0: air, \(n_0 = 1\)
  • Layer 1: coating, \(n_1 \approx \sqrt{n_s}\)
  • Substrate: glass, \(n_s \approx 1.5\)
  • Thickness: \(d = \lambda_0/(4n_1)\)
\[ r = \frac{r_{01}+r_{12}e^{2i\beta}}{1+r_{01}r_{12}e^{2i\beta}}, \qquad \beta = \frac{2\pi n_1 d}{\lambda} \]

At the design wavelength \(\lambda_0\), destructive interference suppresses reflection. Students immediately see how phase thickness becomes a design variable.

Figure 3. TMM multilayer example
A fully teachable first example: simple enough for algebra, useful enough to feel real.
Teaching pay-off: TMM shows that not every EM problem should be thrown into a large 3D solver.

6. Rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA)

Method idea

Periodic gratings

RCWA expands fields and permittivity in Fourier harmonics inside layered periodic media. It is a natural method for diffraction problems, metasurfaces, and grating-mediated resonances.

\[ \varepsilon(x) = \sum_{m=-M}^{M} \varepsilon_m e^{iG_m x}, \qquad G_m = \frac{2\pi m}{\Lambda} \]

Minimal example: 1D dielectric grating

  • Period \(\Lambda\)
  • Fill factor \(f\)
  • Grating height \(h\)
  • Incident plane wave at normal incidence

Typical outputs:

  • 0th-order reflection and transmission
  • diffraction efficiency into \(\pm 1\) orders
  • polarization dependence
  • resonant anomalies from periodicity
High-index contrast and sharp corners often demand careful harmonic convergence. Students must learn that “more orders” is not a cosmetic refinement; it changes the answer.
Figure 4. RCWA grating example
RCWA is the right level of machinery when lateral periodicity matters but full arbitrary 3D complexity is not required.

7. Finite-difference time-domain (FDTD)

Method idea

Broadband time-domain

FDTD updates electric and magnetic fields on a staggered Yee grid. Its defining strength is that one pulse simulation can generate a broadband response after Fourier transformation.

\[ H_z^{n+1/2}\big|_{i+1/2,j+1/2} = H_z^{n-1/2}\big|_{i+1/2,j+1/2} + \frac{\Delta t}{\mu} \left( \frac{E_x^n|_{i+1/2,j+1}-E_x^n|_{i+1/2,j}}{\Delta y} - \frac{E_y^n|_{i+1,j+1/2}-E_y^n|_{i,j+1/2}}{\Delta x} \right) \]
\[ \Delta t \le \frac{1}{c\sqrt{\Delta x^{-2}+\Delta y^{-2}+\Delta z^{-2}}} \]

Minimal example: pulse incident on a dielectric slab

  • 1D or 2D line source launches a Gaussian pulse
  • Wave propagates through free space
  • Part reflects at slab entrance, part transmits
  • Monitors record incident, reflected, and transmitted signals
\[ s(t) = \exp\left[-\left(\frac{t-t_0}{\tau}\right)^2\right]\cos(\omega_0 t) \]

After time stepping, apply FFT to monitor signals to obtain broadband \(R(\omega)\) and \(T(\omega)\). This is where FDTD becomes pedagogically powerful: the student sees the pulse, the reflection delay, and the spectrum from the same run.

In FDTD, the mesh is not just geometric. It controls dispersion, phase error, and the maximum stable time step.

FDTD is organized around staggering in space and time, not around arbitrary CAD elements.

8. Finite element method (FEM)

Method idea

Complex geometry / frequency domain

FEM rewrites Maxwell’s equations in weak form and solves them on unstructured meshes. It excels when geometry is irregular, local refinement matters, or frequency-domain resonances are central.

\[ \nabla \times \mu^{-1}(\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) - \omega^2 \varepsilon \mathbf{E} = i\omega \mathbf{J} \]
\[ \int_{\Omega} (\nabla \times \mathbf{v})\cdot \mu^{-1}(\nabla \times \mathbf{E})\, d\Omega - \omega^2 \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{v}\cdot\varepsilon \mathbf{E}\, d\Omega = i\omega \int_{\Omega} \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{J}\, d\Omega \]

Minimal example: rectangular cavity or waveguide bend

  • Complex shape cannot be represented efficiently by a uniform Cartesian grid
  • Refine mesh only near sharp corners, dielectric interfaces, or ports
  • Solve either a driven frequency response or an eigenvalue problem

A strong classroom example is a 2D waveguide bend: students see that the geometry itself motivates FEM, not brand loyalty to a commercial package.

In FEM, mesh refinement is local. That is the central contrast with FDTD. The page should make this difference unmistakable.

9. Method of moments (MoM)

Method idea

Radiation and conducting surfaces

MoM solves integral equations for unknown currents. It is especially strong for antennas, scattering from conductors, and open-region problems where Green’s functions handle radiation naturally.

\[ \hat{n} \times \mathbf{E}^{\mathrm{inc}} = -\hat{n} \times \int_S \overline{\overline{G}}(\mathbf{r},\mathbf{r}') \cdot \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \, dS' \]
\[ \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}) \approx \sum_{n=1}^{N} I_n \mathbf{f}_n(\mathbf{r}) \]

Minimal example: thin-wire dipole

  • Expand current on short wire segments
  • Impose the electric field integral equation
  • Solve dense matrix system for segment currents
  • From current distribution, derive input impedance and far-field radiation

This is the cleanest classroom bridge between antenna theory and numerical linear algebra.

A compact numerical example that teaches basis expansion, dense systems, and radiation observables.

10. Dispersion and material models

Material modeling is where many simulations quietly fail. Constant \(\varepsilon\) is often inadequate for metals, lossy dielectrics, polar media, and broadband optical problems.

Drude model

\[ \varepsilon(\omega) = \varepsilon_\infty - \frac{\omega_p^2}{\omega^2 + i\gamma \omega} \]

Free-electron metallic response; good first model for noble metals in broad ranges when used carefully.

Lorentz model

\[ \varepsilon(\omega) = \varepsilon_\infty + \sum_j \frac{f_j \omega_j^2}{\omega_j^2-\omega^2-i\gamma_j\omega} \]

Bound resonances, phonons, optical transitions, and many dielectric line-shape fits.

Debye model

\[ \varepsilon(\omega)=\varepsilon_\infty+\frac{\varepsilon_s-\varepsilon_\infty}{1+i\omega\tau} \]

Relaxation response for polar dielectrics and many RF/microwave materials.

Measured data

Often the most defensible choice in engineering work. But students must still inspect interpolation range, units, fitting quality, and measurement conditions.

Do not let the solver hide material ignorance. A reasonable-looking spectrum with the wrong material model is still wrong.

11. Sources, ports, and boundaries

Sources

  • plane wave
  • current dipole
  • Gaussian pulse
  • mode source / wave port

Boundaries

  • PML / CPML for open regions
  • PEC / PMC for ideal symmetries
  • periodic / Bloch for lattices
  • port terminations for guided systems

Port observables

  • \(S_{11}\), \(S_{21}\)
  • phase reference planes
  • impedance / admittance
  • mode purity

These are not implementation details. They define the experiment you are numerically performing. Wrong source placement, wrong port normalization, or poorly separated PML will contaminate the result before post-processing even begins.

12. Practical simulation workflow

Figure 9. Workflow from physics to defensible data
This should mirror the course logic: geometry → mesh → solver → observable, with validation attached to the entire chain.
  1. Define geometry and its scale hierarchy.
  2. Assign physically justified material models.
  3. Select source, ports, and boundary conditions.
  4. Choose the method that matches the problem class.
  5. Choose discretization consistent with the smallest critical scale and wavelength.
  6. Run the solver and extract observables.
  7. Perform convergence, cross-validation, or analytical comparison.

13. Validation and common failure modes

Validation is not a decorative final slide. It is part of the simulation method itself.

Analytical checks

  • plane wave in layered media
  • Mie scattering
  • waveguide cutoff / cavity mode

Convergence checks

  • mesh refinement
  • time-step sensitivity
  • harmonic truncation in RCWA

Cross-validation

  • FDTD vs FEM
  • TMM vs FDTD for layered slab
  • simulation vs measurement
Model failure. Wrong \(\varepsilon\), wrong \(\sigma\), ignored dispersion, or unrealistic geometry.
Discretization failure. Too coarse a mesh, violated CFL, too few RCWA harmonics, poor element quality.
Boundary failure. PML too close, wrong periodicity, incorrect port definition, unintended reflection.
Post-processing failure. Bad FFT window, early time truncation, wrong normalization, wrong phase reference.

Minimal convergence logic

\[ \delta_Q^{(k)} = \frac{|Q_{k+1}-Q_k|}{Q_{k+1}} \]

Replace \(Q\) by any key observable: resonant frequency, \(S_{11}\) minimum, reflectance peak, or cross section. The point is not to run finer meshes forever. The point is to demonstrate that the chosen discretization no longer moves the answer in a consequential way.

14. Two-week mini-labs and resources

Use the PDF as supplementary material. The uploaded Lecture 1 PDF should reinforce the same structure emphasized here: numerical laboratory mindset, workflow from geometry to observable, solver selection, the primacy of observables over raw field plots, and validation/convergence discipline.

Mini-lab 1

TMM slab. Compute \(R(\lambda)\) and \(T(\lambda)\) for a quarter-wave anti-reflection coating. Compare with Fresnel intuition.

Mini-lab 2

FDTD slab. Launch a Gaussian pulse, extract reflection/transmission with FFT, and compare against TMM for the same stack.

Mini-lab 3

FEM resonator. Compute resonant frequency and field localization, then show mesh convergence of the resonance.

Mini-lab 4

RCWA grating. Sweep period or fill factor and watch how diffraction channels open.

Mini-lab 5

MoM dipole. Recover current profile and input impedance near resonance.

Final report template

  • physical problem
  • solver choice justification
  • mesh / time-step logic
  • observable extraction
  • validation evidence
What the student should leave with: not just the ability to run one solver, but the ability to explain why a method was chosen, what it can and cannot resolve, how the observable was extracted, and why the result should be trusted.
