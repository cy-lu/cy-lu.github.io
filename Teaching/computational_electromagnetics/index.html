Computational Electromagnetics

Teaching Notes on Electromagnetic Modeling, Numerical Methods, and Validation

These notes present computational electromagnetics as a numerical laboratory for understanding, predicting, and validating electromagnetic behavior. The focus is not restricted to a single solver. Instead, the aim is to connect Maxwell’s equations, material models, numerical methods, and observable quantities in one coherent workflow.

Working principle. A simulation is not a black box. The correct question is not only “Can the software run?” but also “What physics is being solved, what assumptions are built into the discretization, and what observable can actually be validated?”

1. Why computational electromagnetics matters

Computational electromagnetics (CEM) is the discipline of solving electromagnetic problems numerically. Its role is not limited to making field maps. In practice, CEM is used to:

  • predict field–matter interaction in structures too complex for closed-form analysis,
  • evaluate device performance before fabrication,
  • interpret measurements through model-based analysis, and
  • optimize geometry, materials, and bandwidth before hardware is built.

A good CEM workflow therefore links physical theory, numerical analysis, and engineering observables.

2. Maxwell equations and constitutive relations

The foundation is Maxwell’s equations:

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}, \] \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0. \]

The constitutive relations connect the fields to material response:

\[ \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon \mathbf{E}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H}, \qquad \mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{E}. \]

In optics and wave engineering, the material description is often written using the complex refractive index \(\tilde{n}=n+i\kappa\) or the frequency-dependent permittivity \(\varepsilon(\omega)\).

3. What should be extracted from a simulation?

Raw field data \(|\mathbf{E}|\) or \(|\mathbf{H}|\) is often visually useful but rarely sufficient by itself. The real target is an observable: a quantity that can support interpretation, comparison, or validation.

Common observables include:

  • reflectance \(R\), transmittance \(T\), and absorption \(A\),
  • S-parameters \(S_{11}, S_{21}\),
  • scattering, absorption, and extinction cross sections,
  • quality factor \(Q\), mode volume, and resonance position,
  • field enhancement, power flow, and energy density.
\[ A = 1 - R - T. \]

The difference between a useful simulation and a decorative one is often the difference between field visualization and observable extraction.

4. Main numerical methods

No single numerical method is universally best. The right solver depends on bandwidth, geometry, boundary conditions, resonance behavior, and the type of observable required.

Method Typical strength Typical limitation
TMM Fast for planar multilayers and thin-film stacks Not suitable for laterally patterned structures
FDTD Broadband, transient, full-wave time-domain physics Global grid cost can become very high
FEM Excellent for complex geometry and local mesh refinement Broadband sweeps may require repeated solves
RCWA Periodic layered structures and diffraction problems Less natural for strongly aperiodic geometry

The correct mindset is not “Which method is best?” but “Which method matches the mathematical nature of the problem?”

5. Transfer-matrix method (TMM)

For multilayer media, the transfer-matrix method is often the cleanest starting point. It is efficient, physically transparent, and ideal for teaching interference, impedance matching, and resonant absorption.

TMM is especially useful for:

  • anti-reflection coatings,
  • Fabry–Pérot cavities,
  • metal–dielectric absorber stacks,
  • thin-film reflectance and transmittance analysis.

In your teaching portfolio, this is a strong analytical entry point because it shows that not every EM problem needs a large-scale numerical solver.

6. Finite-difference time-domain (FDTD)

FDTD directly updates electromagnetic fields in time on the Yee grid. Its defining strength is that one transient simulation can produce a broadband response after Fourier analysis.

Key features:

  • explicit time stepping,
  • broadband response from a pulse source,
  • direct access to transient propagation and scattering,
  • clear interpretation of wave travel, reflection, and delay in time.

But FDTD is also demanding. The mesh is not merely geometric; it controls numerical dispersion, stability, and cost. Finer spatial discretization improves fidelity, but it forces a smaller time step.

\[ \Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x}{c\sqrt{n}} \]

This kind of solver-specific constraint is one reason CEM should be taught as a scientific method, not as software operation.

7. Finite element method (FEM)

FEM is often preferable when geometry is complex, dimensions vary strongly, or local refinement is required. In contrast to FDTD, mesh refinement can be concentrated near interfaces, corners, ports, or hotspots.

FEM is particularly strong for:

  • frequency-domain resonators,
  • waveguide and cavity analysis,
  • multiphysics coupling,
  • complex CAD-driven geometry.

In practice, FDTD and FEM should be seen as complementary rather than competing methods.

8. Dispersion and material models

Electromagnetic simulation is only as good as the material model. Constant \(\varepsilon\) is often insufficient for metals, lossy dielectrics, polar media, and broadband optical problems.

Typical models include:

  • Drude model for free-electron metallic behavior,
  • Lorentz oscillators for resonant dielectric response,
  • Debye relaxation for polar materials and dielectrics,
  • tabulated optical constants from measurement.

A visually reasonable spectrum can still be physically wrong if the material model is wrong.

9. A practical CEM workflow

  1. Define geometry and material response
  2. Select the right model: analytical, TMM, FDTD, FEM, or RCWA
  3. Set source conditions, polarization, and boundaries
  4. Choose discretization consistent with wavelength and geometry
  5. Run the solver and extract observables
  6. Verify by benchmark, convergence, or cross-method comparison

This workflow is more important than the software brand. If the workflow is wrong, the results are not trustworthy.

10. Validation and how not to be fooled

Validation is not a decorative final step. It is part of the discipline of simulation itself. A credible result should be checked through one or more of the following:

  • comparison with analytical solutions,
  • mesh or time-step convergence,
  • cross-validation between numerical methods,
  • comparison with experimental data when available.

Common traps include:

  • wrong material parameters,
  • boundary reflections or poor PML placement,
  • insufficient spatial resolution,
  • post-processing errors in FFT and observable extraction.

A good numerical experimentalist is not the one who gets a plot fastest, but the one who knows why the plot should be trusted.

11. Application areas

The same computational EM framework can support a wide range of topics:

  • thin-film optics and multilayer coatings,
  • plasmonics and nanophotonics,
  • metamaterials and effective medium response,
  • antenna design, EMC/EMI, and radar-related problems,
  • scattering, absorption, and resonant energy localization.

In that sense, computational electromagnetics is not just one method. It is a way of organizing physical insight, numerical approximation, and engineering observables into one rigorous workflow.
