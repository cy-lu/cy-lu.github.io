1. Basic definitions

A ply is one thin layer of composite material. When that ply is treated as a mechanically homogeneous orthotropic layer, it is also called a lamina. A laminate is a bonded stack of plies.

  • Ply / lamina: one layer with one material orientation
  • Laminate: the full stack of plies
  • Stacking sequence: the ordered list of ply orientations, such as \([0/45/-45/90]_s\)
  • Ply angle \(\theta_k\): orientation of the \(k\)-th ply relative to the laminate axes
  • Ply thickness \(t_k\): thickness of the \(k\)-th ply

The purpose of CLT is to connect ply-level mechanics to laminate-level response.

Important distinction. In laminate mechanics, we constantly move between two levels:

  • lamina level: what one orthotropic ply does in its own material axes
  • laminate level: what the full bonded stack does in the global plate axes

Many student errors come from mixing these two descriptions. A single ply may be simple in its own material frame, but once that ply is rotated and embedded in a laminate, its contribution to the global response is no longer simple.

2. Why CLT is needed

For an isotropic plate, global stiffness is described by only a few constants. For a laminate, that is no longer enough. The response depends on the ply orientation set \(\{\theta_k\}\), the thickness distribution \(\{t_k\}\), and the lamina orthotropic properties \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\).

CLT is useful because it answers the first structural questions quickly:

  • What is the in-plane stiffness?
  • What is the bending stiffness?
  • Is extension–bending coupling present?
  • Under a given load, what are the mid-plane strains and curvatures?
  • What stress does each ply carry?

Why not jump directly to FEM? Because CLT gives the first mechanical structure of the problem.

Before any finite-element model is built, CLT already tells us whether the laminate is stiff or compliant, symmetric or unsymmetric, strongly coupled or weakly coupled, and which plies are likely to become critical. FEM should extend or validate this understanding, not replace it.

3. Material data and engineering meaning

The basic lamina elastic constants are:

  • \(E_1\): longitudinal Young’s modulus, along the fiber direction
  • \(E_2\): transverse Young’s modulus, normal to the fiber direction
  • \(G_{12}\): in-plane shear modulus
  • \(\nu_{12}\): major Poisson’s ratio

The strength parameters are:

  • \(X_t\), \(X_c\): longitudinal tensile and compressive strengths
  • \(Y_t\), \(Y_c\): transverse tensile and compressive strengths
  • \(S\): in-plane shear strength

Do not mix them. The elastic constants determine deformation and stress distribution. The strength parameters determine whether the predicted stress state is safe or unsafe.

Physical meaning of the material axes.

  • Direction 1 is the fiber direction, usually the stiff and strong direction.
  • Direction 2 is transverse to the fibers in the lamina plane, usually much less stiff and much weaker.

This is why composite plies are strongly anisotropic. The same layer can behave very stiffly in one in-plane direction and much more softly in another. CLT keeps track of this directional behavior as the ply is rotated inside the laminate.

4. Lamina constitutive law

At the lamina level, the first task is to write the constitutive relation of a single orthotropic ply in its own material axes \((1,2)\). These axes are attached to the lamina itself. Direction \(1\) is the fiber direction, and direction \(2\) is the in-plane transverse direction.

In the material axes \((1,2)\), define the in-plane stress and strain vectors as

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12}= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1\\ \sigma_2\\ \tau_{12} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12}= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1\\ \varepsilon_2\\ \gamma_{12} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Each quantity above has a specific mechanical meaning:

  • \(\sigma_1\): normal stress along the fiber direction, units Pa
  • \(\sigma_2\): normal stress transverse to the fiber direction, units Pa
  • \(\tau_{12}\): in-plane shear stress in the lamina material axes, units Pa
  • \(\varepsilon_1\): normal strain along direction \(1\), dimensionless
  • \(\varepsilon_2\): normal strain along direction \(2\), dimensionless
  • \(\gamma_{12}\): engineering shear strain in the \(1\)-\(2\) plane, dimensionless

Important convention. Here \(\gamma_{12}\) is the engineering shear strain, not the tensor shear strain.

In standard laminate theory and in most engineering texts, the constitutive matrix is written using \(\gamma_{12}\), which is twice the tensor shear strain component. This is why the shear modulus appears directly as \(Q_{66}=G_{12}\).

Under plane stress,

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12}=\mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12}, \qquad \mathbf{Q}= \begin{bmatrix} Q_{11} & Q_{12} & 0\\ Q_{12} & Q_{22} & 0\\ 0 & 0 & Q_{66} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The phrase plane stress means that the lamina is assumed thin enough that the out-of-plane stress components are neglected:

\[ \sigma_3=\tau_{13}=\tau_{23}=0. \]

This is a standard assumption in Classical Lamination Theory. It is appropriate for thin laminates when the dominant behavior is in-plane stretching and plate bending, and when interlaminar stresses are not the primary quantity of interest.

What \(\mathbf{Q}\) represents.

\(\mathbf{Q}\) is the reduced stiffness matrix of one lamina in its own material frame. “Reduced” here means reduced from a full three-dimensional orthotropic constitutive law to the two-dimensional plane-stress form used in laminate theory.

The matrix \(\mathbf{Q}\) tells us how much in-plane stress is generated by a given in-plane strain state when the ply is viewed in its own \(1\)-\(2\) axes.

The reciprocal Poisson ratio is

\[ \nu_{21}=\nu_{12}\frac{E_2}{E_1}. \]

This relation is not optional. It follows from the symmetry of the elastic constitutive law. For a linearly elastic orthotropic lamina, the compliance matrix must satisfy a reciprocity condition, and that condition gives the relation above.

The reduced stiffness terms are

\[ Q_{11}=\frac{E_1}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{22}=\frac{E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{12}=\frac{\nu_{12}E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{66}=G_{12}. \]

Their physical meaning should be interpreted carefully:

  • \(Q_{11}\): axial stiffness contribution associated with strain in direction \(1\), units Pa
  • \(Q_{22}\): axial stiffness contribution associated with strain in direction \(2\), units Pa
  • \(Q_{12}\): coupling between normal stress in one material direction and normal strain in the other, units Pa
  • \(Q_{66}\): in-plane shear stiffness, units Pa

Why the zeros appear in \(\mathbf{Q}\).

In the material axes of an orthotropic lamina under plane stress, normal and in-plane shear responses are uncoupled. That is why the \(Q_{16}\) and \(Q_{26}\) terms are zero here. Those coupling terms appear only after the ply is rotated away from its own material axes and expressed in the laminate axes.

It is also useful to distinguish clearly between material constants and stiffness coefficients. The quantities \(E_1\), \(E_2\), \(G_{12}\), and \(\nu_{12}\) are engineering input data. The quantities \(Q_{11}\), \(Q_{22}\), \(Q_{12}\), and \(Q_{66}\) are constitutive coefficients derived from those inputs. In practice, CLT uses \(\mathbf{Q}\), not the raw engineering constants directly.

What this section accomplishes.

Up to this point, we only know the constitutive behavior of one lamina in its own axes. A real laminate, however, is built in the global laminate axes \((x,y)\), and different plies are rotated by different angles. Therefore the next necessary step is to express each ply stiffness in the laminate coordinate system.

5. Coordinate transformation and rotated ply stiffness

A laminate is defined in the global axes \((x,y)\), not in the material axes of every ply. Therefore the ply stiffness must be transformed into the laminate frame.

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}=\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}, \]

where \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) is the transformed reduced stiffness matrix of a ply oriented at angle \(\theta\). This is the step that makes the stacking sequence mechanically meaningful.

In the laminate axes, define

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x\\ \sigma_y\\ \tau_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x\\ \varepsilon_y\\ \gamma_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]
  • \(\sigma_x,\sigma_y\): global normal stresses in the laminate axes, units Pa
  • \(\tau_{xy}\): global in-plane shear stress, units Pa
  • \(\varepsilon_x,\varepsilon_y\): global normal strains, dimensionless
  • \(\gamma_{xy}\): global engineering shear strain, dimensionless
  • \(\theta\): angle from the laminate \(x\)-axis to the lamina \(1\)-axis

In general, the transformed reduced stiffness has the form

\[ \bar{\mathbf{Q}}= \begin{bmatrix} \bar{Q}_{11} & \bar{Q}_{12} & \bar{Q}_{16}\\ \bar{Q}_{12} & \bar{Q}_{22} & \bar{Q}_{26}\\ \bar{Q}_{16} & \bar{Q}_{26} & \bar{Q}_{66} \end{bmatrix}. \]

This matrix is still a stiffness matrix, but now written in the laminate axes. Its entries depend on the original lamina properties and on the ply orientation angle \(\theta\).

Key physical point.

In the material axes, the orthotropic lamina had no normal–shear coupling terms in the constitutive matrix. After rotation, such coupling generally appears through \(\bar{Q}_{16}\) and \(\bar{Q}_{26}\). This is one of the main reasons orientation matters so strongly in laminate mechanics.

The transformed matrix \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) is what is actually assembled through the thickness to form the laminate stiffness matrices \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\). Therefore every ply contributes to the global laminate response through its own rotated stiffness, not through the unrotated \(\mathbf{Q}\) matrix.

Interpretation of special angles.

  • \(0^\circ\) ply: material axes coincide with laminate axes, so \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}=\mathbf{Q}\)
  • \(90^\circ\) ply: fiber and transverse directions are exchanged relative to the laminate frame
  • \(\pm45^\circ\) plies: strong normal–shear coupling effects often appear and these plies are important in shear-dominated response

6. Laminate A, B, and D matrices

Let \(z_0,z_1,\ldots,z_n\) denote the through-thickness interface coordinates. Then the laminate stiffness matrices are

\[ \mathbf{A}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k-z_{k-1}), \] \[ \mathbf{B}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^2-z_{k-1}^2), \] \[ \mathbf{D}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^3-z_{k-1}^3). \]
  • \(\mathbf{A}\): extensional stiffness matrix
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): membrane–bending coupling matrix
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): bending stiffness matrix

If the laminate is symmetric about the mid-plane, \(\mathbf{B}=0\).

The thickness coordinates \(z_k\) are measured through the laminate thickness, usually from the laminate mid-plane. Therefore \(z=0\) is the mid-surface, negative \(z\) lies below it, and positive \(z\) lies above it.

Each matrix has both a mathematical role and a physical meaning:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\) collects the first thickness moment of stiffness and governs in-plane stretching and in-plane shear
  • \(\mathbf{B}\) couples stretching to bending and is the mathematical signature of an unsymmetric laminate
  • \(\mathbf{D}\) collects the bending stiffness contributions weighted more strongly by distance from the mid-plane and governs bending and twisting resistance

Units matter.

  • \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\): units Pa = N/m\(^2\)
  • \(\mathbf{A}\): units N/m
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): units N
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): units N\(\cdot\)m

This unit structure is not accidental. It already tells us that \(\mathbf{A}\) multiplies strains to produce force resultants, \(\mathbf{B}\) couples strains and curvatures to force and moment resultants, and \(\mathbf{D}\) multiplies curvatures to produce moments.

Why symmetric laminates are special.

In a symmetric laminate, the stiffness distribution above and below the mid-plane balances exactly. The odd first moment of stiffness cancels, and therefore \(\mathbf{B}=0\). This means a pure membrane load does not, in the ideal CLT model, induce bending curvature, and a pure bending load does not induce mid-plane strain.

7. Load resultants, mid-plane strain, and curvature

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N}\\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B}\\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Define

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} N_x\\ N_y\\ N_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} M_x\\ M_y\\ M_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \] \[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x^0\\ \varepsilon_y^0\\ \gamma_{xy}^0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\kappa}= \begin{bmatrix} \kappa_x\\ \kappa_y\\ \kappa_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]

This is the main system solved in CLT.

Each of these quantities must be interpreted at the laminate level:

  • \(N_x, N_y\): in-plane normal force resultants, units N/m
  • \(N_{xy}\): in-plane shear force resultant, units N/m
  • \(M_x, M_y\): bending moment resultants per unit width
  • \(M_{xy}\): twisting moment resultant per unit width
  • \(\varepsilon_x^0, \varepsilon_y^0\): mid-plane normal strains, dimensionless
  • \(\gamma_{xy}^0\): mid-plane engineering shear strain, dimensionless
  • \(\kappa_x, \kappa_y\): curvatures in the \(x\) and \(y\) directions, units 1/m
  • \(\kappa_{xy}\): twisting curvature, units 1/m

What “resultant” means.

\(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\) are not point stresses or point moments. They are through-thickness resultants. In other words, they are the net in-plane force and net bending/twisting moment obtained after integrating the stress distribution through the laminate thickness.

What the unknowns are in a forward problem.

In most CLT calculations, the laminate is known first. That means \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\) are already known. The applied loading \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\) is then prescribed. The unknowns solved from the \(6\times 6\) system are the mid-plane strains \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and the curvatures \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\).

Physical interpretation of the coupling block.

If \(\mathbf{B}=0\), stretching and bending are decoupled. If \(\mathbf{B}\neq 0\), they interact. Then a purely in-plane load can produce curvature, and a pure bending moment can produce mid-plane strain. This is the core structural consequence of laminate unsymmetry.

8. Ply stress recovery

Once the laminate mid-plane strain and curvature are known, the next step is to recover the strain and stress in each ply. The in-plane strain at thickness coordinate \(z\) is

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z)=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0+z\boldsymbol{\kappa}. \]

This equation is fundamental. It says that the in-plane strain varies linearly through the thickness in CLT. The mid-plane strain is the reference value at \(z=0\), and the curvature term adds a linear variation away from the mid-plane.

Meaning of each term.

  • \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z)\): global in-plane strain at a particular thickness position \(z\)
  • \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\): mid-plane strain vector
  • \(z\boldsymbol{\kappa}\): strain contribution caused by bending and twisting

This is the plate analogue of the linear strain distribution familiar from elementary beam bending.

The global ply stress is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}^{(k)}(z)=\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z). \]

Here \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\) is the transformed reduced stiffness matrix of the \(k\)-th ply. Therefore the global stress in each ply depends on two things:

  • the local strain state at that thickness position
  • the orientation-dependent stiffness of that ply

For failure criteria, this stress must often be transformed back into the material frame to obtain \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\).

Why stress recovery is not the last step.

The global stresses \(\sigma_x\), \(\sigma_y\), and \(\tau_{xy}\) are useful for understanding laminate-level response, but lamina failure criteria are usually written in the material axes of each ply. Therefore a stress state that looks modest in the laminate frame can still be critical when resolved into \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\) in the ply frame.

Practical implementation detail.

Stress is often recovered at the top and bottom surfaces of each ply, because those are the locations where bending-induced extremes usually occur. If only one value per ply is reported, important peak stresses can be missed.

9. Strength and first-ply failure

A simple failure indicator is the Tsai–Hill failure index

\[ FI= \left(\frac{\sigma_1}{X}\right)^2 -\frac{\sigma_1\sigma_2}{X^2} +\left(\frac{\sigma_2}{Y}\right)^2 +\left(\frac{\tau_{12}}{S}\right)^2. \]

Here \(X\) and \(Y\) are selected from tensile or compressive strength values according to the sign of \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\).

The symbols in the criterion mean:

  • \(\sigma_1\): normal stress along the fiber direction
  • \(\sigma_2\): normal stress transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(\tau_{12}\): in-plane shear stress in the ply material axes
  • \(X\): longitudinal strength used for the sign of \(\sigma_1\)
  • \(Y\): transverse strength used for the sign of \(\sigma_2\)
  • \(S\): in-plane shear strength
  • \(FI\): failure index, dimensionless

How to select \(X\) and \(Y\).

  • If \(\sigma_1>0\), use \(X=X_t\)
  • If \(\sigma_1<0\), use \(X=X_c\)
  • If \(\sigma_2>0\), use \(Y=Y_t\)
  • If \(\sigma_2<0\), use \(Y=Y_c\)

This sign-dependent choice matters because composite laminae usually have very different tensile and compressive strengths, especially in the transverse direction.

Interpretation of the index.

  • \(FI<1\): the ply is predicted to remain below the criterion surface
  • \(FI=1\): the ply reaches the criterion surface
  • \(FI>1\): the ply is predicted to fail according to the chosen criterion

In a laminate calculation, the first ply that reaches \(FI=1\) defines the first-ply failure load.

Limitation to remember.

Tsai–Hill is a convenient quadratic interaction criterion, but it does not explicitly distinguish different physical failure modes such as fiber tension, fiber compression, matrix cracking, or shear-driven failure. It is useful for teaching and first screening, but it should not be mistaken for a full damage model.

10. Worked laminate example

Use representative carbon/epoxy lamina data:

QuantityValue
\(E_1\)135 GPa
\(E_2\)10 GPa
\(G_{12}\)5 GPa
\(\nu_{12}\)0.30
\(X_t\)1500 MPa
\(X_c\)1200 MPa
\(Y_t\)40 MPa
\(Y_c\)150 MPa
\(S\)70 MPa

Laminate:

\[ [0/45/-45/90]_s,\qquad t_{ply}=0.125~\text{mm} \]

Applied load:

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} 1000\\ 0\\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{N/m},\qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Why this example is useful.

The laminate \([0/45/-45/90]_s\) is symmetric, so it is a good first benchmark. A correct implementation should give \(\mathbf{B}\approx 0\), and under pure in-plane loading the computed curvatures should be negligible. That makes this example a good debugging case for sign conventions, transformation formulas, and ABD assembly.

11. MATLAB implementation

11.1 Main script

E1  = 135e9;  E2  = 10e9;  G12 = 5e9;  v12 = 0.30;
Xt = 1500e6;  Xc = 1200e6; Yt = 40e6;  Yc = 150e6; S = 70e6;

angles = [0 45 -45 90 90 -45 45 0];
tply   = 0.125e-3;
NM     = [1000;0;0;0;0;0];

[ABD, z, Qbar_all] = build_laminate_ABD(E1,E2,G12,v12,angles,tply);

ek    = ABD \ NM;
eps0  = ek(1:3);
kappa = ek(4:6);

11.2 Function: build laminate ABD

function [ABD, z, Qbar_all] = build_laminate_ABD(E1,E2,G12,v12,angles,tply)
v21 = v12 * E2 / E1;
den = 1 - v12*v21;

Q11 = E1 / den;   Q22 = E2 / den;
Q12 = v12 * E2 / den;   Q66 = G12;

nply = length(angles);
h = nply * tply;
z = linspace(-h/2, h/2, nply+1);

A = zeros(3,3); B = zeros(3,3); D = zeros(3,3);
Qbar_all = zeros(3,3,nply);

for k = 1:nply
    theta = angles(k);
    m = cosd(theta); n = sind(theta);

    Qbar11 = Q11*m^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*n^4;
    Qbar22 = Q11*n^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*m^4;
    Qbar12 = (Q11+Q22-4*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q12*(m^4+n^4);
    Qbar16 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3;
    Qbar26 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3 - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n;
    Qbar66 = (Q11+Q22-2*Q12-2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q66*(m^4+n^4);

    Qbar = [Qbar11 Qbar12 Qbar16;
            Qbar12 Qbar22 Qbar26;
            Qbar16 Qbar26 Qbar66];

    Qbar_all(:,:,k) = Qbar;

    zk1 = z(k); zk2 = z(k+1);
    A = A + Qbar * (zk2-zk1);
    B = B + 0.5 * Qbar * (zk2^2-zk1^2);
    D = D + (1/3) * Qbar * (zk2^3-zk1^3);
end

ABD = [A B; B D];
end

11.3 Function: transform global stress to ply axes

function sigma_12 = stress_xy_to_12(sigma_xy, theta)
m = cosd(theta); n = sind(theta);
sx = sigma_xy(1); sy = sigma_xy(2); txy = sigma_xy(3);

s1  = m^2*sx + n^2*sy + 2*m*n*txy;
s2  = n^2*sx + m^2*sy - 2*m*n*txy;
t12 = -m*n*sx + m*n*sy + (m^2-n^2)*txy;

sigma_12 = [s1; s2; t12];
end

How the code maps onto the theory.

  • The material constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\) define the lamina constitutive law.
  • The loop over angles computes the transformed stiffness of each ply.
  • The thickness coordinates z define the ply interfaces.
  • The matrices A, B, and D are assembled by thickness integration.
  • The vector ek contains \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\).

So the MATLAB implementation follows the theory in exactly the same order as the teaching notes: lamina stiffness \(\rightarrow\) rotated ply stiffness \(\rightarrow\) laminate ABD \(\rightarrow\) laminate response.

12. Example numerical results and how to read them

For the symmetric laminate \([0/45/-45/90]_s\) under pure in-plane loading, the first structural checks are:

  1. Is \(\mathbf{B}\) numerically close to zero?
  2. Are the curvatures \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) negligible under pure membrane loading?
  3. Which plies carry the highest longitudinal stress \(\sigma_1\)?
  4. Which plies carry the highest transverse stress \(\sigma_2\) or shear stress \(\tau_{12}\)?
  5. Which ply gives the largest failure index \(FI\)?

Typical interpretation. In a symmetric laminate under pure in-plane loading, one expects \(\mathbf{B}\approx 0\), and therefore the induced curvature should also be very small. If the computed \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) is unexpectedly large, the first things to check are the stacking sequence, the sign convention, and the ABD assembly.

The stress distribution should also be interpreted physically:

  • Plies oriented close to \(0^\circ\) usually carry more of the axial load in the fiber direction.
  • \(\pm45^\circ\) plies often carry more shear-related response.
  • \(90^\circ\) plies are more sensitive to transverse stress and often become critical earlier in transverse failure criteria.

This is exactly why the stress must be transformed back to the ply axes before any strength statement is made.

What would indicate a bug or a misunderstanding?

  • A clearly symmetric laminate giving a large \(\mathbf{B}\) matrix
  • Pure membrane loading giving unexpectedly large curvature
  • \(0^\circ\) plies not carrying a significant fraction of axial load under \(N_x\)
  • failure indices that are obviously inconsistent with the stress pattern

These are not subtle issues. They usually point to errors in angle convention, coordinate transformation, ply ordering, thickness coordinates, or unit conversion.

13. Recommended plots and outputs

A good teaching script should not end with raw console output alone. The following outputs are particularly useful:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\) matrices printed clearly
  • mid-plane strain vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\)
  • curvature vector \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)
  • bar chart of \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\) for each ply
  • bar chart of failure index \(FI\) for each ply

Recommended plot logic:

  1. Plot ply number on the horizontal axis.
  2. Plot \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), or \(\tau_{12}\) on the vertical axis.
  3. Use a separate plot for the failure index \(FI\).
  4. Mark the critical ply directly in the figure caption or title.

These plots make the code pedagogically useful, because they connect laminate theory to interpretable structural behavior.

Teaching recommendation.

If this page is used in class, do not show only the final failure index. Show the intermediate layers of reasoning: first the laminate stiffness, then the solved mid-plane response, then the recovered ply stresses, and only then the failure measure. Students learn laminate mechanics much better when they see the chain of cause and effect.
