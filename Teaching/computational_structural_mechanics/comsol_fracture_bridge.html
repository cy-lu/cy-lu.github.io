COMSOL Validation and Fracture Bridge

How CLT, strength assessment, and finite element analysis fit together

1. Why COMSOL is needed after CLT

CLT gives the global laminate mechanics efficiently. It tells us the expected extensional stiffness, bending stiffness, membrane–bending coupling, mid-plane strain, curvature, and ply-level in-plane stress.

But CLT does not resolve local geometry. It does not know about:

  • holes and cutouts,
  • free-edge stress concentrations,
  • local constraints and fixtures,
  • complex thermal gradients,
  • interlaminar stress peaks at interfaces.

That is where COMSOL enters. The correct way to use it is not to replace CLT, but to validate and extend the CLT-based understanding.

2. What should be compared between CLT and COMSOL?

If CLT is the baseline, then COMSOL should first be checked against quantities that CLT actually predicts:

  • mid-plane strain \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\),
  • curvature \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\),
  • global displacement trend,
  • ply-level in-plane stress trend.
\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N}\\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B}\\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix} \]

If these global responses disagree strongly in a simple benchmark plate, then the COMSOL model setup should be questioned before moving on to more complicated geometry.

3. A practical COMSOL workflow

  1. Build a simple benchmark geometry first, such as a rectangular laminate plate.
  2. Select a structural formulation appropriate for the problem: shell or solid mechanics.
  3. Define orthotropic ply properties \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\).
  4. Assign ply angles \(\theta_k\) and thicknesses \(t_k\).
  5. Apply loads equivalent to \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\).
  6. Solve the benchmark and compare the global response with CLT.
  7. Only then move on to local features such as holes, free edges, notches, or thermal mismatch.

Teaching principle. Start from the problem that CLT can solve. If the FEM model cannot reproduce the CLT benchmark, then more complicated results should not yet be trusted.

4. Strength versus fracture

It is important not to confuse strength-based failure prediction with fracture mechanics.

Strength-based criteria, such as Tsai–Hill, answer the question:

Is the stress state in a ply large enough to indicate failure onset?

Fracture mechanics answers a different question:

If damage exists or starts, how does it propagate?

The difference is fundamental:

  • Strength criteria predict onset.
  • Fracture mechanics studies growth.

5. Why local stress concentration matters

In CLT, the stress field is smooth through the laminate model. But in a real structure, holes, cutouts, corners, and free edges can produce strong local stress concentration.

These local peaks matter because they often trigger:

  • matrix cracking,
  • fiber-dominated failure,
  • delamination initiation,
  • interface failure between adjacent layers.

A common workflow is therefore:

  1. Use CLT to understand the global laminate response.
  2. Use FEM to identify local hotspot regions.
  3. Use strength criteria for first-pass screening.
  4. Use fracture mechanics if propagation is the real design concern.

6. Bridge to fracture mechanics

The natural fracture-mechanics bridge from laminate analysis includes:

  • matrix crack initiation and growth,
  • free-edge delamination,
  • interface crack growth,
  • energy release rate \(G\),
  • mode-mixity and cohesive-zone concepts.

The key conceptual transition is:

CLT and strength criteria tell you where failure is likely to begin. Fracture mechanics tells you how damage evolves once it has begun.

That is why it is pedagogically correct to learn laminate stiffness and ply stress first, then failure criteria, and only after that move into delamination and fracture.

7. Recommended learning order

  1. Understand ply and laminate definitions
  2. Understand \(\mathbf{Q}\), \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\), \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\)
  3. Implement the CLT workflow in MATLAB
  4. Check ply-level strength criteria
  5. Build a benchmark COMSOL model
  6. Move to local stress concentration problems
  7. Only then enter fracture mechanics
