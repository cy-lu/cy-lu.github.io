Computational Structural Mechanics

Analytical Modeling, Finite Element Simulation, and Design Optimization

This module presents a practical workflow for structural modeling using analytical methods, MATLAB implementation, and COMSOL Multiphysics. The emphasis is on layered and heterogeneous structures, where stiffness, stress, coupling, and reliability depend on both material properties and structural configuration.

1. Why structural modeling matters

Many engineering systems consist of multiple materials, bonded layers, or anisotropic components. Their mechanical response depends not only on geometry and loading, but also on elastic constants, layer thickness, and spatial arrangement. In such systems, analytical modeling provides fast physical insight, while finite element simulation resolves geometry-dependent fields and local stress concentrations.

2. Classical Lamination Theory (CLT)

Classical Lamination Theory is a compact analytical framework for predicting the in-plane and bending response of laminated structures. Each lamina is described in its material coordinates \((1,2)\) by the in-plane stresses \((\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\tau_{12})\) and strains \((\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2,\gamma_{12})\).

The constitutive law of a lamina is written as

[σ1 σ2 τ12]ᵀ = [Q][ε1 ε2 γ12]ᵀ

where \(E_1\) is the longitudinal Young’s modulus, \(E_2\) is the transverse Young’s modulus, \(G_{12}\) is the in-plane shear modulus, and \(\nu_{12}\) is the major Poisson’s ratio.

After rotation by the ply angle \(\theta\), the reduced stiffness matrix \(\mathbf{Q}\) is transformed into the laminate coordinates \((x,y)\), giving the transformed reduced stiffness \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\).

The laminate constitutive equation is

[N M]ᵀ = [A B; B D][ε0 κ]ᵀ

where \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\) are the in-plane force and moment resultants, \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) is the mid-plane strain vector, and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) is the curvature vector. The matrices \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\) represent extensional stiffness, bending-extension coupling, and bending stiffness, respectively.

3. MATLAB implementation

In MATLAB, the workflow is organized into four steps:

  1. Define lamina properties \((E_1, E_2, G_{12}, \nu_{12})\)
  2. Construct \(\mathbf{Q}\) and \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) for each ply
  3. Assemble the laminate stiffness matrices \(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{D}\)
  4. Solve for \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\), \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\), and recover ply stresses

4. COMSOL-based finite element simulation

COMSOL Multiphysics is used to validate analytical predictions and to extend the analysis to complex geometries, realistic boundary conditions, thermal stress, and local field concentrations.

A typical workflow includes:

  1. Build the geometry
  2. Select the structural interface (Shell or Solid Mechanics)
  3. Assign layered or anisotropic material properties
  4. Apply loads and boundary conditions
  5. Generate mesh and solve
  6. Compare global deformation and local stress with analytical predictions

For layered structures, CLT provides the global baseline, while COMSOL resolves local stress gradients, geometric effects, thermal expansion mismatch, and failure-prone regions.

5. Optimization

Once the analytical and numerical models are established, design variables such as ply angle \(\theta\), layer thickness \(t\), material selection, and geometry can be optimized. Typical objectives include stiffness maximization, weight reduction, stress control, and reliability improvement.

6. Failure and reliability

Structural analysis is incomplete without failure assessment. Depending on the application, relevant failure mechanisms may include matrix cracking, fiber-dominated failure, interfacial separation, and delamination. These are evaluated using stress recovery, failure criteria, and, when needed, higher-fidelity simulation.
