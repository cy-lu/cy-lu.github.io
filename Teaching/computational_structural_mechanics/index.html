Computational Structural Mechanics

Teaching Notes on Classical Lamination Theory, MATLAB, COMSOL, and the Bridge to Fracture Mechanics

This module is about how to model a laminated structure in a way that is physically meaningful, computationally transparent, and useful for engineering interpretation. The subject is not only “how to compute a matrix,” but how to understand a complete workflow:

material data \(\rightarrow\) lamina stiffness \(\rightarrow\) laminate stiffness \(\rightarrow\) deformation \(\rightarrow\) ply stress \(\rightarrow\) strength assessment \(\rightarrow\) FEM validation \(\rightarrow\) fracture-mechanics extension

The central analytical tool is Classical Lamination Theory (CLT). CLT starts from the mechanics of a single ply, builds the laminate stiffness from all plies, and then predicts the response under in-plane forces and bending moments. MATLAB is used here because it makes the computational sequence explicit. COMSOL is used later because it resolves geometry-dependent effects and local stress concentrations that lie outside simple plate theory.

1. What problem is being solved here?

A laminate is a stack of thin layers, or plies, each with its own material orientation \(\theta_k\) and thickness \(t_k\). Even when every ply comes from the same material system, the laminate does not behave like a simple isotropic plate. Its stiffness depends on:

  • the lamina elastic constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\),
  • the stacking sequence \([\theta_1/\theta_2/\cdots/\theta_n]\),
  • the ply thicknesses \(t_k\),
  • whether the laminate is symmetric or unsymmetric.

The practical engineering questions are:

  1. How stiff is the laminate in membrane loading?
  2. How stiff is it in bending?
  3. Does the laminate exhibit membrane–bending coupling?
  4. Under a given load, what strains and curvatures develop?
  5. Which ply sees the highest stress, and is that stress still below its strength limit?

2. Why is this not just a “composites” topic?

Laminate mechanics is not merely a materials topic. It is fundamentally a structural mechanics topic, because the real objective is not just to list material constants, but to predict the behavior of a built structure under load. A change in ply angle or stacking sequence changes:

  • the extensional stiffness,
  • the bending stiffness,
  • the coupling behavior,
  • the stress carried by each ply,
  • the likelihood of failure initiation.

This is why CLT belongs naturally inside a broader computational structural mechanics framework.

3. The central constitutive statement

The key equation of CLT is

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N}\\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B}\\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Here:

  • \(\mathbf{N}=[N_x,N_y,N_{xy}]^T\): in-plane force resultants per unit width
  • \(\mathbf{M}=[M_x,M_y,M_{xy}]^T\): bending and twisting moment resultants per unit width
  • \(\mathbf{A}\): extensional stiffness matrix
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): membrane–bending coupling matrix
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): bending stiffness matrix
  • \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0=[\varepsilon_x^0,\varepsilon_y^0,\gamma_{xy}^0]^T\): mid-plane strain vector
  • \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}=[\kappa_x,\kappa_y,\kappa_{xy}]^T\): curvature vector

This equation is not the start of the subject by accident. It is the point toward which all earlier definitions are moving: ply stiffness, coordinate transformation, and through-thickness integration all exist in order to build \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\).

4. What has to be understood before fracture mechanics?

Fracture mechanics is not the first topic in this module because it is not the first question in the analysis. Before asking how a crack propagates, one must first know:

  • what the laminate stiffness is,
  • how the laminate deforms,
  • what stress each ply carries,
  • whether the stress level suggests failure onset.

In other words:

laminate stiffness and ply stress come first; fracture and damage growth come later.

That order is not simplification for convenience. It is the correct physical order for learning and for modeling.

5. How these notes are organized

This module is split into two pages because the subject has two natural levels.

  1. CLT Theory and MATLAB: this is the core page. It defines ply, lamina, laminate, material properties, laminate matrices, strength parameters, and the main MATLAB implementation.
  2. COMSOL Validation and Fracture Bridge: this is the engineering extension. It explains what COMSOL adds, what should be checked against CLT, and how the discussion naturally leads toward fracture mechanics.

6. Recommended reading order

  1. Start with CLT Theory and MATLAB if you want to understand the mechanics from the foundation upward.
  2. Then read COMSOL Validation and Fracture Bridge to connect the theory to finite element analysis and to prepare for later damage-related topics.

The first page is the conceptual and computational core. The second page should be read as the simulation and interpretation extension.
