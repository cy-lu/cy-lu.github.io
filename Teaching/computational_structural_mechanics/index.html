Computational Structural Mechanics

Teaching Notes on Classical Lamination Theory, MATLAB Implementation, Strength Assessment, and COMSOL-Based Validation

These notes are written as a practical learning path. The goal is not only to derive Classical Lamination Theory (CLT), but to connect it to a real engineering workflow: material data → laminate stiffness → deformation → ply stress → strength check → FEM validation → fracture-mechanics bridge.

If this page is used correctly, it should answer three questions clearly:

  • What is being modeled? A laminate made of anisotropic plies with known elastic and strength properties.
  • Why is it being modeled? To predict stiffness, deformation, and the stress state in each ply before building a higher-fidelity FEM model.
  • What comes next? Strength assessment, local stress concentration analysis, and finally damage or fracture mechanics when failure progression matters.

1. Why laminate mechanics matters

A laminate is not just a thicker plate. Its response depends on the ply elastic constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\), the ply strengths \((X_t,X_c,Y_t,Y_c,S)\), the orientation angles \(\theta_k\), the ply thicknesses \(t_k\), and the stacking sequence itself.

That means two laminates made from the same raw material can behave very differently. A \([0/0/0/0]\) laminate is not mechanically equivalent to \([0/45/-45/90]\), even if every ply comes from the same prepreg system.

In practical design, one usually wants to know:

  • How stiff is the laminate in membrane loading?
  • How stiff is it in bending?
  • Will in-plane loading generate bending because of coupling?
  • Which ply sees the largest stress?
  • Is the stress still below the ply strength?

CLT answers the global mechanics efficiently. It is therefore the correct first model before more detailed COMSOL analysis.

2. The full workflow

Material data \(\rightarrow\) Lamina stiffness \(\rightarrow\) Ply rotation \(\rightarrow\) Laminate stiffness \(\rightarrow\) Load response \(\rightarrow\) Ply stress \(\rightarrow\) Strength check \(\rightarrow\) FEM validation \(\rightarrow\) Fracture / damage mechanics

This order matters. If the laminate stiffness and ply stress are not understood first, then later steps such as failure prediction, interlaminar damage, or fracture mechanics are harder to interpret.

3. CLT assumptions and limits

Classical Lamination Theory is based on the following assumptions:

  • each ply is linearly elastic,
  • the laminate is thin relative to its in-plane dimensions,
  • normals to the laminate mid-surface remain straight and normal after deformation,
  • transverse shear deformation is neglected,
  • bonding between plies is perfect.

These assumptions make CLT powerful for global response, but also define its limits. CLT is strong for:

  • membrane stiffness,
  • bending stiffness,
  • extension–bending coupling,
  • ply-level in-plane stress recovery.

CLT is not the right final model for:

  • interlaminar stress concentrations,
  • thick laminates with large transverse shear,
  • holes, free edges, and local geometric discontinuities,
  • damage growth, delamination propagation, or crack-tip fields.

4. Where material parameters come from

The first question students usually ask is correct: where do \(E_1\), \(E_2\), \(G_{12}\), \(\nu_{12}\), \(X_t\), \(X_c\), \(Y_t\), \(Y_c\), and \(S\) come from?

In practice, there are three common sources:

  1. Manufacturer datasheets or published lamina data. This is the most direct and usually the best starting point.
  2. Micromechanics estimates. If only fiber and matrix data are available, the lamina properties can be estimated.
  3. Experimental back-calculation. Coupon tests or laminate tests can be used to infer effective properties.

4.1 Elastic constants

The lamina stiffness parameters are:

  • \(E_1\): Young’s modulus along the fiber direction
  • \(E_2\): Young’s modulus transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(G_{12}\): in-plane shear modulus
  • \(\nu_{12}\): major Poisson’s ratio

4.2 Strength parameters

The lamina strength parameters are:

  • \(X_t\): longitudinal tensile strength
  • \(X_c\): longitudinal compressive strength
  • \(Y_t\): transverse tensile strength
  • \(Y_c\): transverse compressive strength
  • \(S\): in-plane shear strength

Important distinction. The elastic constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\) control deformation and stress distribution. The strength parameters \((X_t,X_c,Y_t,Y_c,S)\) determine whether the ply is likely to fail.

4.3 Micromechanics example

If only fiber and matrix properties are available, the simplest estimate for the longitudinal modulus is the rule of mixtures:

\[ E_1 \approx V_f E_f + (1-V_f)E_m, \]

where \(V_f\) is the fiber volume fraction, \(E_f\) is the fiber modulus, and \(E_m\) is the matrix modulus. This is usually acceptable for \(E_1\), but not sufficient for all other constants.

In a teaching example, it is acceptable to use representative lamina values, as long as it is stated clearly that a real design must use data from a specific material system.

5. Lamina constitutive law

Consider one orthotropic lamina in its material axes \((1,2)\). The in-plane stress vector and strain vector are

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ \sigma_2 \\ \tau_{12} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 \\ \varepsilon_2 \\ \gamma_{12} \end{bmatrix}. \]
  • \(\sigma_1\): normal stress along the fiber direction
  • \(\sigma_2\): normal stress transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(\tau_{12}\): in-plane shear stress in the material frame
  • \(\varepsilon_1\): normal strain along the fiber direction
  • \(\varepsilon_2\): normal strain transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(\gamma_{12}\): engineering shear strain

Under plane stress, the constitutive relation is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \mathbf{Q}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12}, \qquad \mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} Q_{11} & Q_{12} & 0 \\ Q_{12} & Q_{22} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & Q_{66} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The reciprocal Poisson ratio is

\[ \nu_{21}=\nu_{12}\frac{E_2}{E_1}. \]

The reduced stiffness terms are

\[ Q_{11}=\frac{E_1}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{22}=\frac{E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{12}=\frac{\nu_{12}E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{66}=G_{12}. \]

At this stage, \(\mathbf{Q}\) is still the stiffness of one ply in its own material frame.

6. Ply rotation and transformed stiffness

In a real laminate, plies are oriented at angles \(\theta_k\) relative to the laminate axes \((x,y)\). Therefore the ply stiffness must be rotated from the material frame \((1,2)\) to the global laminate frame.

The global in-plane stress and strain vectors are

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x \\ \sigma_y \\ \tau_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x \\ \varepsilon_y \\ \gamma_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The rotated constitutive law is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}=\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}, \]

where \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) is the transformed reduced stiffness matrix.

Physical meaning. \(\mathbf{Q}\) tells you how one ply behaves in its own principal directions. \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) tells the laminate how that same ply contributes after being rotated by \(\theta\).

7. Laminate stiffness: \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), \(\mathbf{D}\)

Let the laminate have \(n\) plies and interface coordinates \(z_0,z_1,\dots,z_n\), measured from the mid-plane. Then the laminate stiffness matrices are

\[ \mathbf{A}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k-z_{k-1}), \] \[ \mathbf{B}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^2-z_{k-1}^2), \] \[ \mathbf{D}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^3-z_{k-1}^3). \]
  • \(\mathbf{A}\): extensional stiffness matrix
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): bending–extension coupling matrix
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): bending stiffness matrix

Their meanings are not interchangeable:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\) controls how in-plane loads produce in-plane strains.
  • \(\mathbf{D}\) controls how bending moments produce curvatures.
  • \(\mathbf{B}\) couples membrane response and bending response.

If the laminate is symmetric, then \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{0}\). If it is not symmetric, then in-plane loading can generate curvature.

8. Mid-plane strain \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and curvature \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)

The laminate constitutive equation is written in resultant form:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N} \\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 \\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The force and moment resultants are

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} N_x \\ N_y \\ N_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} M_x \\ M_y \\ M_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The generalized deformations are

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x^0 \\ \varepsilon_y^0 \\ \gamma_{xy}^0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\kappa}= \begin{bmatrix} \kappa_x \\ \kappa_y \\ \kappa_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]
  • \(N_x,N_y,N_{xy}\): in-plane force resultants per unit width
  • \(M_x,M_y,M_{xy}\): bending/twisting moment resultants per unit width
  • \(\varepsilon_x^0,\varepsilon_y^0,\gamma_{xy}^0\): mid-plane strains
  • \(\kappa_x,\kappa_y,\kappa_{xy}\): curvatures

This is the central system solved in CLT.

9. Ply strain and ply stress recovery

Once \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) are known, the global strain at any thickness coordinate \(z\) is

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z)=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 + z\boldsymbol{\kappa}. \]

For the \(k\)-th ply,

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}^{(k)}(z)= \bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z). \]

If the stress is needed in the material frame, transform back to

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12}^{(k)}= \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ \sigma_2 \\ \tau_{12} \end{bmatrix}. \]

This step matters because most lamina strength criteria are written in terms of \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\), not \(\sigma_x\), \(\sigma_y\), and \(\tau_{xy}\).

10. Worked example: carbon/epoxy quasi-isotropic laminate

Consider a representative carbon/epoxy lamina with the following illustrative properties:

Quantity Meaning Value
\(E_1\)longitudinal modulus135 GPa
\(E_2\)transverse modulus10 GPa
\(G_{12}\)in-plane shear modulus5 GPa
\(\nu_{12}\)major Poisson ratio0.30
\(X_t\)longitudinal tensile strength1500 MPa
\(X_c\)longitudinal compressive strength1200 MPa
\(Y_t\)transverse tensile strength40 MPa
\(Y_c\)transverse compressive strength150 MPa
\(S\)in-plane shear strength70 MPa

Use the laminate

\[ [0/45/-45/90]_s \]

with uniform ply thickness

\[ t_{ply}=0.125~\text{mm}. \]

Apply the in-plane load resultant

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} 1000 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \text{N/m}, \qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \text{N}. \]

This example answers five concrete questions:

  1. What are \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\)?
  2. Is \(\mathbf{B}=0\) for this symmetric laminate?
  3. What are \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)?
  4. Which plies carry the largest \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\)?
  5. Is any ply close to its strength limit?

Why this example is useful. It is simple enough for a first code, but realistic enough to show how laminate stiffness, ply angle, stress transformation, and strength assessment fit together.

11. Strength parameters and failure criteria

Stress alone is not the final answer. The engineering question is whether the stress state is still acceptable relative to the ply strengths.

A simple first-ply failure criterion is the Tsai–Hill index:

\[ FI= \left(\frac{\sigma_1}{X}\right)^2 -\frac{\sigma_1\sigma_2}{X^2} +\left(\frac{\sigma_2}{Y}\right)^2 +\left(\frac{\tau_{12}}{S}\right)^2. \]

Here \(X\) and \(Y\) must be chosen using the correct tensile or compressive strengths depending on the sign of \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\):

  • If \(\sigma_1 \ge 0\), use \(X=X_t\); if \(\sigma_1 < 0\), use \(X=X_c\).
  • If \(\sigma_2 \ge 0\), use \(Y=Y_t\); if \(\sigma_2 < 0\), use \(Y=Y_c\).

Interpretation:

  • \(FI < 1\): predicted safe
  • \(FI = 1\): predicted failure onset
  • \(FI > 1\): predicted first-ply failure

This is the point where global laminate mechanics connects to material failure.

12. MATLAB implementation

The code should reflect the mechanics, not hide it. A clean MATLAB implementation should follow the sequence:

  1. Define lamina stiffness parameters and strength parameters
  2. Build \(\mathbf{Q}\)
  3. For each ply angle \(\theta_k\), build \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\)
  4. Assemble \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\)
  5. Apply \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\)
  6. Solve for \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)
  7. Recover ply stresses
  8. Compute failure index \(FI\)

12.1 Main example script

% Representative carbon/epoxy lamina data
E1  = 135e9;      % Pa
E2  = 10e9;       % Pa
G12 = 5e9;        % Pa
v12 = 0.30;

Xt = 1500e6;      % Pa
Xc = 1200e6;      % Pa
Yt = 40e6;        % Pa
Yc = 150e6;       % Pa
S  = 70e6;        % Pa

angles = [0 45 -45 90 90 -45 45 0];
tply   = 0.125e-3;     % m

NM = [1000; 0; 0; 0; 0; 0];   % [Nx Ny Nxy Mx My Mxy]^T

[ABD, z, Qbar_all] = build_laminate_ABD(E1,E2,G12,v12,angles,tply);

ek = ABD \ NM;
eps0  = ek(1:3);
kappa = ek(4:6);

fprintf('eps0 =\\n');
disp(eps0)

fprintf('kappa =\\n');
disp(kappa)

for k = 1:length(angles)
    zmid = 0.5*(z(k)+z(k+1));
    eps_xy = eps0 + zmid*kappa;

    sigma_xy = Qbar_all(:,:,k) * eps_xy;
    sigma_12 = stress_xy_to_12(sigma_xy, angles(k));

    s1 = sigma_12(1);
    s2 = sigma_12(2);
    t12 = sigma_12(3);

    if s1 >= 0
        X = Xt;
    else
        X = Xc;
    end

    if s2 >= 0
        Y = Yt;
    else
        Y = Yc;
    end

    FI = (s1/X)^2 - (s1*s2)/(X^2) + (s2/Y)^2 + (t12/S)^2;

    fprintf('Ply %d, angle = %+g deg\\n', k, angles(k));
    fprintf('sigma_12 = [%.3e %.3e %.3e] Pa\\n', s1, s2, t12);
    fprintf('Tsai-Hill FI = %.4f\\n\\n', FI);
end

12.2 Function: build laminate ABD

function [ABD, z, Qbar_all] = build_laminate_ABD(E1,E2,G12,v12,angles,tply)

v21 = v12 * E2 / E1;
den = 1 - v12*v21;

Q11 = E1 / den;
Q22 = E2 / den;
Q12 = v12 * E2 / den;
Q66 = G12;

Q = [Q11 Q12 0;
     Q12 Q22 0;
     0   0   Q66];

nply = length(angles);
h = nply * tply;
z = linspace(-h/2, h/2, nply+1);

A = zeros(3,3);
B = zeros(3,3);
D = zeros(3,3);

Qbar_all = zeros(3,3,nply);

for k = 1:nply
    theta = angles(k);
    m = cosd(theta);
    n = sind(theta);

    Qbar11 = Q11*m^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*n^4;
    Qbar22 = Q11*n^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*m^4;
    Qbar12 = (Q11+Q22-4*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q12*(m^4+n^4);
    Qbar16 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3;
    Qbar26 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3 - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n;
    Qbar66 = (Q11+Q22-2*Q12-2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q66*(m^4+n^4);

    Qbar = [Qbar11 Qbar12 Qbar16;
            Qbar12 Qbar22 Qbar26;
            Qbar16 Qbar26 Qbar66];

    Qbar_all(:,:,k) = Qbar;

    zk1 = z(k);
    zk2 = z(k+1);

    A = A + Qbar * (zk2 - zk1);
    B = B + 0.5 * Qbar * (zk2^2 - zk1^2);
    D = D + (1/3) * Qbar * (zk2^3 - zk1^3);
end

ABD = [A B;
       B D];
end

12.3 Function: transform global stress back to ply axes

function sigma_12 = stress_xy_to_12(sigma_xy, theta)

m = cosd(theta);
n = sind(theta);

sx  = sigma_xy(1);
sy  = sigma_xy(2);
txy = sigma_xy(3);

s1  = m^2*sx + n^2*sy + 2*m*n*txy;
s2  = n^2*sx + m^2*sy - 2*m*n*txy;
t12 = -m*n*sx + m*n*sy + (m^2-n^2)*txy;

sigma_12 = [s1; s2; t12];
end

This code is intentionally explicit. It is easier to learn from a transparent script first, and only then refactor it into a larger toolbox.

13. COMSOL validation

Once the analytical baseline is clear, COMSOL should be used to validate or extend the analysis. A clean validation workflow is:

  1. Build the laminate geometry or use a layered shell representation
  2. Define anisotropic material constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\)
  3. Assign ply angles \(\theta_k\) and thicknesses \(t_k\)
  4. Apply loads equivalent to \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\)
  5. Compare global deformation with CLT predictions
  6. Check ply-level stresses where possible

COMSOL is not there to replace CLT. It is there to answer the next level of questions:

  • What happens near holes or free edges?
  • How large are the local stress concentrations?
  • What happens when thermal loading is added?
  • Where does the stress state depart from simple plate-theory assumptions?

14. Bridge to fracture mechanics

At the end of a CLT analysis, one usually knows:

  • the global laminate stiffness,
  • the deformation under load,
  • the stress state in each ply,
  • which ply is most likely to fail first.

But CLT plus a first-ply failure criterion is not yet fracture mechanics.

Fracture mechanics becomes necessary when the question changes from “Is the ply likely to fail?” to “How does damage initiate and propagate?”

Examples:

  • matrix crack growth,
  • free-edge delamination,
  • interface cracking,
  • energy release rate \(G\),
  • cohesive-zone or VCCT-based delamination modeling.

Correct progression. Learn laminate stiffness first. Then learn ply stress and strength. Then move to local stress concentration and FEM. Only after that should fracture and damage growth be introduced.
