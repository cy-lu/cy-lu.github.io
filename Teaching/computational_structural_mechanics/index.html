Computational Structural Mechanics

Teaching Notes on Analytical Modeling, MATLAB Implementation, and COMSOL-Based Simulation

These notes introduce a practical workflow for structural modeling of layered and anisotropic systems. The emphasis is on clear physical definitions, compact derivations, and simulation-oriented interpretation. Classical Lamination Theory (CLT) is used as the analytical foundation, while MATLAB and COMSOL are treated as complementary tools for computation and validation.

1. Why this topic matters

Many engineering structures are not made from a single isotropic material. Instead, they contain multiple bonded layers, directional materials, thin films, substrates, stiffeners, or laminated sections. In such systems, stiffness and stress are controlled not only by geometry, but also by material orientation, layer thickness, and stacking sequence.

For these problems, it is useful to separate the workflow into two levels:

  • Analytical modeling: fast prediction of global stiffness, force–deformation relations, and stress trends
  • Finite element simulation: resolution of realistic geometry, boundary conditions, local stress gradients, and coupled physics

In these notes, the analytical model is Classical Lamination Theory. It does not replace finite element analysis, but it gives the physical baseline that should be understood before building a COMSOL model.

2. Lamina constitutive law

Consider a single orthotropic lamina in its material coordinates \((1,2)\). The in-plane stress vector is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ \sigma_2 \\ \tau_{12} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 \\ \varepsilon_2 \\ \gamma_{12} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Under plane stress, the constitutive relation is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \mathbf{Q}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12}, \] where \[ \mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} Q_{11} & Q_{12} & 0 \\ Q_{12} & Q_{22} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & Q_{66} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The material parameters are:

  • Longitudinal Young’s modulus \(E_1\)
  • Transverse Young’s modulus \(E_2\)
  • In-plane shear modulus \(G_{12}\)
  • Major Poisson’s ratio \(\nu_{12}\)

The reciprocal Poisson’s ratio is

\[ \nu_{21} = \nu_{12}\frac{E_2}{E_1}. \]

The reduced stiffness entries are

\[ Q_{11}=\frac{E_1}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{22}=\frac{E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{12}=\frac{\nu_{12}E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{66}=G_{12}. \]

This matrix describes the lamina only in the material axes. Once the ply is rotated in a laminate, the stiffness must be transformed into the laminate coordinates.

3. Ply rotation and transformed stiffness

Let the ply be rotated by an angle \(\theta\) from the laminate coordinates \((x,y)\) to the material coordinates \((1,2)\). Then the reduced stiffness \(\mathbf{Q}\) becomes the transformed reduced stiffness \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\), which operates in the laminate frame.

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy} = \bar{\mathbf{Q}}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}. \]

Physically:

  • \(\mathbf{Q}\): stiffness in the ply’s own material axes
  • \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\): stiffness seen by the laminate in global coordinates

This transformation is the key reason that stacking sequence matters. A laminate made from identical plies can behave very differently depending on the orientation set \(\{\theta_k\}\).

4. Laminate stiffness: \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), \(\mathbf{D}\)

Let the laminate consist of \(n\) plies, with thickness coordinates \(z_0, z_1, \dots, z_n\), measured from the laminate mid-plane. The extensional, coupling, and bending stiffnesses are

\[ \mathbf{A}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k-z_{k-1}), \] \[ \mathbf{B}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^2-z_{k-1}^2), \] \[ \mathbf{D}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^3-z_{k-1}^3). \]

Their meanings are:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\): in-plane extensional stiffness
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): bending–extension coupling stiffness
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): bending stiffness

A symmetric laminate about the mid-plane has \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{0}\). If \(\mathbf{B}\neq\mathbf{0}\), membrane loading can generate curvature, and bending can generate in-plane strain.

5. Mid-plane strain \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and curvature \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)

The laminate constitutive equation is written in resultant form:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N} \\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 \\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Here,

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} N_x \\ N_y \\ N_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} M_x \\ M_y \\ M_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \] \[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x^0 \\ \varepsilon_y^0 \\ \gamma_{xy}^0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\kappa}= \begin{bmatrix} \kappa_x \\ \kappa_y \\ \kappa_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Once \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\) are assembled, the unknown mid-plane strain and curvature follow from inversion of the laminate stiffness matrix. This is the central CLT prediction step.

6. Ply stress recovery

The in-plane strain at any thickness coordinate \(z\) is

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(z)=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 + z\boldsymbol{\kappa}. \]

For the \(k\)-th ply, the stress in laminate coordinates is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}^{(k)} = \bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(z). \]

If needed, this stress can be transformed back to material coordinates to obtain \(\sigma_1\), \(\sigma_2\), and \(\tau_{12}\). This step is important whenever failure criteria are defined in the ply axes rather than in the global laminate frame.

7. MATLAB workflow

A practical MATLAB implementation usually follows these steps:

  1. Input material constants \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\)
  2. Build \(\mathbf{Q}\) for a single lamina
  3. Generate \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\) for each ply angle \(\theta_k\)
  4. Assemble \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\)
  5. Solve for \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)
  6. Recover ply stresses

8. COMSOL workflow

After the analytical baseline is clear, COMSOL can be used to resolve geometry-dependent and boundary-dependent effects. A clean workflow is:

  1. Build the geometry
  2. Select Shell or Solid Mechanics, depending on the structural level
  3. Assign layered or anisotropic material properties
  4. Apply boundary conditions and loads
  5. Mesh and solve
  6. Compare global response against CLT

The analytical model gives the expected global response. COMSOL then adds:

  • finite geometry effects
  • local stress concentration
  • thermal strain and thermal mismatch
  • coupled-field effects when required

In other words, CLT is the fast physical model, while COMSOL is the higher-fidelity structural model.

9. Optimization and next steps

Once the analytical and numerical workflows are established, the same framework can be extended to design studies. Typical design variables include ply angle \(\theta\), layer thickness \(t\), geometry, and material selection. Typical objectives include stiffness increase, warpage reduction, stress control, and robustness under thermal loading.

Failure and reliability do matter, but at this stage they only need to be placed in context: first understand constitutive behavior and stiffness, then verify with simulation, and only then move toward damage, delamination, or interface reliability.
