Teaching Notes on Classical Lamination Theory, MATLAB Implementation, and COMSOL-Based Validation

These notes are written for learning how a laminate model actually works. The goal is not to present CLT as a black-box formula set, but to show how the mechanical quantities are defined, how they are assembled into the laminate stiffness, and how the same workflow can be implemented in MATLAB and then checked in COMSOL.

1. Why this topic matters

A laminated structure is built from plies with different orientations \(\theta_k\), thicknesses \(t_k\), and material properties \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\). Even if all plies are made from the same material, the global stiffness changes when the stacking sequence changes.

The two main questions are:

  • Given a laminate, what are its in-plane and bending stiffnesses?
  • Given a load, what are the strains, curvatures, and ply stresses?

Classical Lamination Theory answers these questions quickly. It is therefore the right starting point before building a more detailed finite element model.

2. CLT assumptions and where it is valid

CLT is based on the following assumptions:

  • each ply is linearly elastic,
  • the laminate is thin compared with its in-plane dimensions,
  • normals to the mid-surface remain straight and normal after deformation,
  • transverse shear deformation is neglected,
  • bonding between plies is perfect.

As a result, CLT is best for global stiffness, membrane-bending coupling, and ply-level in-plane stress trends. It is not a complete model for interlaminar stress, thick laminates, or local damage.

3. Lamina constitutive law

Consider one orthotropic lamina in its material axes \((1,2)\). The in-plane stress vector is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ \sigma_2 \\ \tau_{12} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 \\ \varepsilon_2 \\ \gamma_{12} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Here:

  • \(\sigma_1\): normal stress along the fiber direction
  • \(\sigma_2\): normal stress transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(\tau_{12}\): in-plane shear stress in the material frame
  • \(\varepsilon_1\): normal strain along the fiber direction
  • \(\varepsilon_2\): normal strain transverse to the fiber direction
  • \(\gamma_{12}\): engineering shear strain in the material frame

Under plane stress, the constitutive law is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12} = \mathbf{Q}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{12}, \qquad \mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} Q_{11} & Q_{12} & 0 \\ Q_{12} & Q_{22} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & Q_{66} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The material constants are:

  • \(E_1\): longitudinal Young’s modulus
  • \(E_2\): transverse Young’s modulus
  • \(G_{12}\): in-plane shear modulus
  • \(\nu_{12}\): major Poisson’s ratio
  • \(\nu_{21}=\nu_{12}E_2/E_1\): reciprocal Poisson’s ratio
\[ Q_{11}=\frac{E_1}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{22}=\frac{E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{12}=\frac{\nu_{12}E_2}{1-\nu_{12}\nu_{21}},\qquad Q_{66}=G_{12}. \]

This \(\mathbf{Q}\) matrix is defined only in the material frame. A laminate, however, is assembled in the global laminate frame \((x,y)\), so each ply stiffness must be rotated.

4. Ply rotation and transformed stiffness

Let a ply be oriented at angle \(\theta\) relative to the laminate axes \((x,y)\). After rotation, the constitutive law becomes

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy} = \bar{\mathbf{Q}}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}, \]

where

  • \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}=[\sigma_x,\sigma_y,\tau_{xy}]^T\)
  • \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}=[\varepsilon_x,\varepsilon_y,\gamma_{xy}]^T\)
  • \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\): transformed reduced stiffness matrix in the laminate frame

The physical meaning is simple:

  • \(\mathbf{Q}\) describes one ply in its own principal directions.
  • \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}\) tells the laminate how that ply contributes in the global \(x\)–\(y\) system.

This is why two laminates made from the same plies can have very different global behavior if their angle set \(\{\theta_k\}\) is different.

5. Laminate stiffness: \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), \(\mathbf{D}\)

For a laminate with \(n\) plies, let \(z_0,z_1,\dots,z_n\) be the through-thickness coordinates of the ply interfaces, measured from the laminate mid-plane. Then the laminate stiffness matrices are

\[ \mathbf{A}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k-z_{k-1}), \] \[ \mathbf{B}=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^2-z_{k-1}^2), \] \[ \mathbf{D}=\frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}(z_k^3-z_{k-1}^3). \]

The three matrices represent:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\): extensional stiffness
  • \(\mathbf{B}\): bending–extension coupling stiffness
  • \(\mathbf{D}\): bending stiffness

Their units are different, and their roles are different:

  • \(\mathbf{A}\) relates in-plane forces to mid-plane strains
  • \(\mathbf{B}\) couples in-plane forces with curvature, and moments with mid-plane strains
  • \(\mathbf{D}\) relates bending moments to curvature

If the laminate is symmetric about the mid-plane, then \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{0}\). If \(\mathbf{B}\neq\mathbf{0}\), a purely in-plane load may induce bending.

6. Mid-plane strain \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and curvature \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)

The laminate constitutive equation is written in resultant form as

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{N} \\ \mathbf{M} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{A} & \mathbf{B} \\ \mathbf{B} & \mathbf{D} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 \\ \boldsymbol{\kappa} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The vectors are:

\[ \mathbf{N}= \begin{bmatrix} N_x \\ N_y \\ N_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{M}= \begin{bmatrix} M_x \\ M_y \\ M_{xy} \end{bmatrix}, \] \[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_x^0 \\ \varepsilon_y^0 \\ \gamma_{xy}^0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\kappa}= \begin{bmatrix} \kappa_x \\ \kappa_y \\ \kappa_{xy} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Here:

  • \(N_x,N_y,N_{xy}\): in-plane force resultants per unit width
  • \(M_x,M_y,M_{xy}\): bending/twisting moment resultants per unit width
  • \(\varepsilon_x^0,\varepsilon_y^0,\gamma_{xy}^0\): mid-plane strains
  • \(\kappa_x,\kappa_y,\kappa_{xy}\): curvatures and twisting curvature

Once the \(6\times6\) laminate stiffness matrix is assembled, the unknowns \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) are found by inversion.

7. Ply strain and ply stress recovery

After solving for \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\), the strain at thickness position \(z\) is

\[ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z)=\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0 + z\boldsymbol{\kappa}. \]

For the \(k\)-th ply, the global in-plane stress is

\[ \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{xy}^{(k)}(z)= \bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z). \]

If the stress is needed in the material axes, then transform back to \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{12}^{(k)}=[\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\tau_{12}]^T\). This is usually the stress vector used in ply-level failure criteria.

In practice, a common choice is to evaluate stress at the top and bottom of each ply, because stress varies through thickness whenever \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\neq\mathbf{0}\).

8. MATLAB workflow: what each step does

A clean MATLAB implementation is not just a script that dumps numbers. It is a sequence of physically meaningful steps:

  1. Define material properties \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\)
  2. Build \(\mathbf{Q}\) for one lamina
  3. For each ply angle \(\theta_k\), compute \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\)
  4. Build interface coordinates \(z_k\) from the ply thicknesses \(t_k\)
  5. Assemble \(\mathbf{A}\), \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\mathbf{D}\)
  6. Apply the laminate loads \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{M}\)
  7. Solve for \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\)
  8. Recover ply strains and stresses

That sequence is the heart of CLT in code.

9. MATLAB example code

The following compact example shows the actual computational flow. It is not a full toolbox, but it makes the logic explicit. 

% Material properties of one orthotropic ply
E1  = 135e9;
E2  = 10e9;
G12 = 5e9;
v12 = 0.30;
v21 = v12 * E2 / E1;

% Reduced stiffness matrix Q
den = 1 - v12 * v21;
Q11 = E1 / den;
Q22 = E2 / den;
Q12 = v12 * E2 / den;
Q66 = G12;

Q = [Q11 Q12 0;
     Q12 Q22 0;
     0   0   Q66];

% Laminate definition
angles = [0 45 -45 90 90 -45 45 0];
nply   = numel(angles);
tply   = 0.125e-3;
h      = nply * tply;

% Through-thickness coordinates z_k
z = linspace(-h/2, h/2, nply+1);

% Initialize laminate stiffness matrices
A = zeros(3,3);
B = zeros(3,3);
D = zeros(3,3);

for k = 1:nply
    theta = angles(k);
    m = cosd(theta);
    n = sind(theta);

    % Transformed reduced stiffness Qbar
    Qbar11 = Q11*m^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*n^4;
    Qbar22 = Q11*n^4 + 2*(Q12+2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q22*m^4;
    Qbar12 = (Q11+Q22-4*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q12*(m^4+n^4);
    Qbar16 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3;
    Qbar26 = (Q11-Q12-2*Q66)*m*n^3 - (Q22-Q12-2*Q66)*m^3*n;
    Qbar66 = (Q11+Q22-2*Q12-2*Q66)*m^2*n^2 + Q66*(m^4+n^4);

    Qbar = [Qbar11 Qbar12 Qbar16;
            Qbar12 Qbar22 Qbar26;
            Qbar16 Qbar26 Qbar66];

    zk1 = z(k);
    zk2 = z(k+1);

    A = A + Qbar * (zk2 - zk1);
    B = B + 0.5 * Qbar * (zk2^2 - zk1^2);
    D = D + (1/3) * Qbar * (zk2^3 - zk1^3);
end

ABD = [A B; B D];

% Resultant load vector [Nx Ny Nxy Mx My Mxy]^T
NM = [1000; 0; 0; 0; 0; 0];

% Solve for [eps0; kappa]
ek = ABD \ NM;
eps0  = ek(1:3);
kappa = ek(4:6);

disp('Mid-plane strain eps0 =');
disp(eps0)

disp('Curvature kappa =');
disp(kappa)

Once \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\kappa}\) are known, the stress in each ply can be recovered by evaluating \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{xy}(z)\) and then applying \(\bar{\mathbf{Q}}^{(k)}\). 

% Example: stress recovery at the mid-surface of ply k
k = 1;
zmid = 0.5 * (z(k) + z(k+1));
eps_xy = eps0 + zmid * kappa;
sigma_xy = Qbar * eps_xy;

In a better organized code, the stress-recovery step should be placed in its own function so that each ply is treated consistently.

10. COMSOL workflow

After the analytical baseline is clear, COMSOL should be used to answer questions that CLT cannot answer reliably. A practical workflow is:

  1. Build the geometry
  2. Select Shell or Solid Mechanics
  3. Define anisotropic material properties \((E_1,E_2,G_{12},\nu_{12})\)
  4. Assign ply orientations \(\theta_k\) and thicknesses \(t_k\)
  5. Apply boundary conditions and loads \((\mathbf{N},\mathbf{M})\) or equivalent edge loads
  6. Mesh and solve
  7. Compare global response against CLT

CLT should be used as the first physical check. COMSOL should then be used to capture finite geometry, local stress concentration, and coupled-field behavior that lies outside the CLT assumptions.

11. Optimization and next steps

Once the analytical and numerical workflows are established, the same framework can be extended to design studies. Typical design variables are:

  • ply angle \(\theta_k\),
  • ply thickness \(t_k\),
  • stacking sequence,
  • material selection,
  • geometric dimensions.

Typical objectives include higher extensional stiffness, lower warpage, smaller peak stress, or improved robustness under thermal loading.

Failure and reliability should be introduced only after the stiffness and stress workflow is clear. Otherwise the analysis becomes more complicated before the basic mechanics is understood.
